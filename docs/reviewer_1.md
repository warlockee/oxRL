The "Instruct vs. Base" Confound (You Disproved Your Own Thesis)The paper claims to study how "post-training algorithms differ," but the primary scaling experiments are conducted on Qwen 2.5 Instruct models."Instruct" models have already been heavily post-trained (SFT + RLHF) by the Qwen team. Applying DPO/RL to them is studying "continual alignment drift" or domain-specific over-fitting, not fundamental post-training.The Smoking Gun: In Section 5.4, you reveal that when you initialize from the Base model, the massive 15.7 pp performance spread between algorithms completely collapses to a statistically negligible 1.07 pp.Conclusion: Your title asks, "Do Post-Training Algorithms Actually Differ?" Your own ablation proves the answer is No. The massive "scale-dependent ranking inversions" you base your entire paper around are likely just artifacts of how destructively different loss functions interact with Qwen's proprietary instruct weights. You buried your most important finding (that initialization matters more than the algorithm) in an ablation, while basing your abstract on the confounded Instruct data.2. The 1.5B Logical ParadoxThe paper’s two main conclusions actively destroy each other:Claim A: Algorithm rankings invert across scale. You explicitly warn: "Rankings at $\le$ 3B do not predict 7B behavior" (e.g., SimPO goes from worst at 1.5B to tied-best at 7B).Claim B: Loss function variants don't matter. None of the 20 DPO variants beat vanilla DPO, so practitioners should "use vanilla DPO; no variant improves upon it."The Flaw: You only tested the 20 DPO variants at 1.5B! If Claim A is true, then 1.5B is a completely unreliable proxy for deployment-scale efficacy. How do we know one of those 20 variants doesn't experience the exact same "scale-inversion" that SimPO did and massively outperform vanilla DPO at 7B? You cannot use 1.5B as a graveyard to dismiss 20 algorithms while simultaneously warning the reader that 1.5B results are scale-fragile.3. Statistical Hypocrisy at 7B ($N=2$)You rightfully scold the ML community for ignoring seed variance, dedicating an entire section to the PyTorch sampler bug. Yet, at 7B—where your core "ranking inversion" claim rests—you evaluate SFT, DPO, and SimPO on exactly 2 seeds, and IPO/KTO on 1 seed.You state that SimPO and DPO are "statistically indistinguishable ($p>0.05$)" at 7B. This is a classic statistical fallacy. Failing to reject the null hypothesis with an $N=2$ and a massive standard deviation ($\sigma=2.16$ for SimPO) does not prove equivalence; it just means you have zero statistical power. You cannot preach about seed variance and then base your primary conclusion on $N=2$.4. You Are Measuring Regex Formatting, Not "Alignment"The paper makes sweeping claims about post-training alignment, but evaluates almost entirely on GSM8K exact-match.As you note in Table 10, the 3B model has a 52.2 pp gap between strict match and flexible extraction. This proves your benchmark is largely measuring format compliance (whether the loss function successfully forces the model to output #### [Answer]), not actual reasoning or human preference alignment (tone, helpfulness, safety).When evaluated on general domains (ARC, HellaSwag), the algorithm spread collapses to $< 0.5$ pp. Claiming that "algorithms behave differently" when they actually just learn strict formatting regexes at different speeds is a vast overstatement.5.
