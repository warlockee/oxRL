NeurIPS demands rigorous statistical inference, broad experimental validation, and clear generalizability—this paper falls short on all three, with gaps that undermine the credibility of its key conclusions (even if the findings are likely correct). The weaknesses are not minor typos or missing analyses—they are foundational limitations to the study’s experimental design and statistical power.
1. Severe Statistical Underpowering (Most Damaging Weakness)
7B scale only has N=2 seeds for the core comparison (SFT/DPO/SimPO); IPO/KTO are single-seed. This makes formal statistical comparison impossible for the paper’s most important result (the 7B preference method dominance). The authors acknowledge overlapping confidence intervals but cannot rule out chance as the cause of SimPO/DPO differences—NeurIPS requires empirical claims to be statistically validated. A sample size of N=2 is unacceptable for a flagship empirical study; it leaves the 7B ranking inversion (the paper’s "striking" scale result) as a provisional observation, not a conclusion.
3B scale suffers from a floor effect (strict-match accuracy ≤18.35%) that invalidates the LoRA/scale factorial analysis. The authors admit this but do not attempt to mitigate it (e.g., flexible extraction for 3B, additional seeds, or a 4B/5B scale to avoid the floor)—the 3B results are therefore interpretable only for format compliance, not algorithm performance.
DPO variant null result is only at 1.5B: The 0/20 variant finding is a key contribution but is strictly a small-scale result. The authors note computational limits prevent 7B runs but do not provide even indirect evidence (e.g., Base initialization at 7B, hyperparameter sweeps) to support generalizability—this leaves the result as a narrow observation, not a general claim about DPO variants.
2. Narrow Experimental Scope Limits Generalizability
Single model family (Qwen 2.5): The study uses only Qwen 2.5 (0.5B–7B) —no other model families (Llama, Mistral, GPT-NeoX) are tested. A core claim of the paper is that "initialization dominates algorithm choice"—but this is untested on other Instruct/Base model pairs, which could have different alignment properties. NeurIPS requires empirical results to be reproducible across model families (not just a single model) to avoid model-specific artifacts.
Synthetic preference data only: All preference training uses model self-play on GSM8K—no human preference data is tested. The paper acknowledges this but does not address how human preferences (the real-world use case for RLHF/DPO) might alter algorithm rankings (e.g., human preferences for tone/safety vs. model self-play for math format). This is a fatal gap for a paper about LLM alignment algorithms—alignment is defined by human preferences, not synthetic self-play.
Math reasoning only (with format compliance as a confound): The study focuses exclusively on GSM8K/MATH (mathematical reasoning with exact-match scoring), with the core GSM8K spread driven by format compliance, not reasoning ability. The authors note the results may not generalize to open-ended tasks (tone/safety/helpfulness) but do not test any open-ended task—this leaves the paper’s conclusions as task-specific, not general to LLM post-training.
Greedy decoding only: All evaluations use greedy decoding—no sampling (top-k, top-p) is tested. Sampling is standard in real-world LLM deployment, and algorithm performance (e.g., online RL’s rollout quality) could differ drastically with sampling—this limits the practical relevance of the results.
3. Unaddressed Methodological and Interpretive Gaps
Online RL compute cost vs. performance: The paper notes SGRPO has 6× higher compute cost at 1.5B but does not perform a systematic compute-performance tradeoff analysis (e.g., GPU-hours per pp gain across scales, early stopping for online RL). This leaves practitioners without clear guidance on when online RL is worth the compute—an essential detail for a paper targeting practical use.
Format compliance as a mediator: The paper correctly identifies format compliance as the main driver of GSM8K spread but does not isolate reasoning ability from format compliance (e.g., using a format-agnostic math evaluation metric, fine-tuning on format-only data first). This makes it impossible to answer the critical question: do these post-training algorithms improve reasoning, or just format adherence?
LoRA vs. full FT at 7B: The 7B scale uses LoRA (r=16, α=32) while ≤3B uses full FT—the authors do not test full FT at 7B (citing compute limits). While the 3B LoRA/full FT factorial suggests scale drives the inversion, the lack of 7B full FT means the paper cannot rule out LoRA regularization as a confounding factor for the 7B preference method dominance.
Seed variance interpretation: The paper documents high seed variance for SimPO at 7B (σ=2.16) but does not investigate the cause (e.g., training dynamics, data ordering, reference-free loss instability). This leaves the variance as an unexplained artifact, not a mechanistic finding.
4. Minor but Persistent Writing/Structural Issues
Overclaiming provisional results: The paper refers to the 7B SimPO/DPO "ranking inversion" as a finding—but with N=2 seeds, it is a trend. NeurIPS penalizes overclaiming statistical results, and this undermines the paper’s credibility.
Missing appendices for key analyses: Some critical analyses (e.g., format compliance for general-domain tasks, online RL training dynamics at 7B) are referenced but not fully presented in the appendices—this makes reproducibility harder for the community.
Unclear definition of "Base/Instruct initialization": The paper does not provide a precise definition of Qwen 2.5’s Base vs. Instruct checkpoints (e.g., pre-training data, alignment steps, instruction tuning details)—this is essential for replicating the initialization ablation, a core result.
Verdict: Reject (Strong Revise-Resubmit)
This paper is not ready for NeurIPS acceptance in its current form due to severe statistical underpowering, a narrow experimental scope, and unaddressed generalizability gaps. However, it is a top-tier revise-resubmit candidate—the core findings are novel, the OXRL framework is a community resource, and the methodological rigor (when executed fully) sets a new standard for LLM post-training research. A revised version that addresses the critical weaknesses below would be a strong NeurIPS accept (likely even a spotlight) due to its transformative impact on the field.
Mandatory Revision Roadmap (To Achieve Acceptance)
The authors must address all of the following (not just a subset) to meet NeurIPS’s bar—these are non-negotiable for rigorous empirical research:
Fix statistical underpowering:
Add N≥5 seeds for 7B SFT/DPO/SimPO (the minimum for meaningful t-tests/ANOVA) and N≥3 seeds for IPO/KTO.
Mitigate the 3B floor effect (e.g., flexible extraction for algorithm performance comparison, add a 4B/5B Qwen 2.5 scale to avoid the floor).
Run a small DPO variant sweep (top 5 variants) at 7B (with N=3 seeds) to support generalizability of the 1.5B null result.
Broaden experimental scope for generalizability:
Test at least one additional model family (e.g., Llama 3 8B) with Base/Instruct checkpoints to validate the initialization dominance result.
Add human preference data (e.g., GSM8K human preference pairs from the literature) to test algorithm performance on real-world alignment.
Evaluate one open-ended task (e.g., MT-Bench for helpfulness, SafetyBench for safety) to test generalizability beyond math reasoning.
Add sampling-based evaluation (top-k=50, top-p=0.9) to reflect real-world LLM deployment.
Isolate reasoning from format compliance:
Develop a format-agnostic math evaluation metric (e.g., using a verifier to score reasoning steps independent of output format) to test whether post-training algorithms improve reasoning or just format adherence.
Run a format-only pre-training ablation (fine-tune all algorithms on GSM8K format data first) to quantify the format compliance contribution to accuracy.
Address key methodological gaps:
Test 7B full FT (at least N=2 seeds) to rule out LoRA regularization as a confound for the 7B preference method dominance.
Perform a systematic compute-performance tradeoff analysis (GPU-hours per pp gain, early stopping) for online RL across scales to guide practitioner choices.
Investigate the cause of SimPO’s 7B seed variance (e.g., training loss dynamics, data ordering sweeps, reference-free loss stability) and report mechanistic findings.
Tone down overclaims and improve writing:
Revise all claims about 7B/SimPO/DPO to reflect provisionality (e.g., "trend" instead of "finding") until N≥5 seeds are added.
Add precise definitions of Qwen 2.5 Base/Instruct checkpoints and full details of synthetic self-play preference data generation.
Expand appendices to include all critical analyses (format compliance for general tasks, online RL training dynamics, seed variance decomposition).
