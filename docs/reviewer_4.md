As a picky reviewer, I look closely at whether the experimental design actually supports the sweeping claims in the abstract. Unfortunately, the core comparative claims ("rankings invert across scale", "offline beats online") are invalidated by three fatal methodological flaws:1. The "Fixed Hyperparameter" Fallacy (Invalidates SFT results)In an attempt to be "controlled," the authors proudly fix the learning rate to $1 \times 10^{-6}$ for all algorithms across all scales (Section 4, Table 10). This is a critical experimental error.At $\le$ 1.5B, the authors use Full Fine-Tuning. At 7B, they use LoRA.It is standard deep learning knowledge that LoRA requires a substantially higher learning rate (typically $10^{-4}$ to $2 \times 10^{-4}$) than Full-FT. By forcing LoRA SFT to use a microscopic $10^{-6}$ LR, you essentially starved the SFT model.This is glaringly obvious in Table 2: at 1.5B, SFT gains +23.2 pp over the base model. At 7B, SFT gains only +1.6 pp. The conclusion that "SFT collapses from best offline method to worst at 7B" is almost certainly an artifact of severe under-training, not a fundamental scaling law.2. The Online RL Baseline is Sabotaged ($\lambda_{KL} = 0$)The paper claims that "offline preference methods overtake online RL at 7B." However, Table 10 explicitly states that SGRPO was trained with a KL penalty of $\lambda_{KL} = 0$.In online RL for LLMs, the KL penalty against the reference model is the primary mechanism preventing policy degradation and reward hacking. Running GRPO without a KL penalty is a fundamentally broken baseline. The authors unwittingly admit this in Section 5.3, noting that 7B SGRPO accuracy "slightly degrades with continued training."Comparing optimally-tuned DPO ($\beta=0.1$, which includes an implicit KL penalty) against unregularized online RL is a strawman comparison.3. The LoRA vs. Full-FT ConfoundThe central narrative is that "rankings invert across scale." However, the inversion happens at the exact same boundary where the tuning paradigm switches from Full-FT (3B) to LoRA (7B). The authors tried to disentangle this with a $2\times2$ factorial at 3B (Table 1), but openly admit it failed due to a "floor effect." Therefore, the independent variable (scale) is perfectly confounded with the optimization architecture (LoRA). You cannot claim scale caused the inversion; it is equally likely that DPO just handles the low-rank regularization of LoRA better at a $10^{-6}$ learning rate than SFT does.4. Logical Contradiction in the Initialization ClaimThe profound claim that "initialization dominates algorithm choice" is based entirely on the ablation in Table 5, which is only performed at 1.5B. The entire rest of the paper is dedicated to proving that 1.5B results do not predict 7B results! It is logically inconsistent to claim that scale fundamentally alters algorithm behavior, but then base your central thesis about initialization entirely on a small-scale model.5. Overclaimed ScopeThe title "Do Post-Training Algorithms Actually Differ?" implies a general finding for LLM alignment. However, this study strictly evaluates deterministic mathematical reasoning using synthetic self-play rewards. Aligning a model for open-ended human aesthetics (tone, helpfulness, safety) involves highly noisy, multi-dimensional reward landscapes where these algorithms behave vastly differently. The title and claims must be scoped down.📝 Questions for the Rebuttal / Conditions to Raise My Score:I want to accept this paper because the insights regarding format compliance and DPO variance are excellent.
